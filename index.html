Levels of Shared Autonomy in Brain-Robot Interfaces

Enabling Multi-Human Multi-Robot Collaboration for Activities of Daily Living

Project Overview

Abstract

Individuals with ALS and other severe motor impairments often rely on caregivers for daily tasks, which limits their independence and sense of control. Brain-robot interfaces (BRIs) have the potential to restore autonomy, but existing systems are often task-specific and highly automated.

In this work, we introduce a collaborative BRI that integrates non-invasive EEG, EMG, and eye tracking to enable multi-user, multi-robot interaction in a shared kitchen environment. Our system supports three levels of autonomy, balancing user control and robotic assistance.

Results from our study show that intermediate autonomy provided the highest task success and fastest completion times while maintaining user agency and trust.

Motivation

We aim to advance assistive robotics to empower people using wheelchairs in their daily lives. By enabling intuitive collaboration between humans and robots, Coop OpenEnded explores how technology can support independence and improve quality of life.

Assistive Kitchen

Our experiments take place in a simulated assistive kitchen, a testbed designed to study how people and robots collaborate in everyday tasks. The environment includes a central table, storage areas, and common objects such as cubes, utensils, and containers, arranged to mimic a realistic household setup.

Within this setting, participants and robots work together on pick-and-place tasks, such as moving objects from one area to another, organizing items on the table, or retrieving them from storage. These tasks allow us to evaluate different forms of human input and robot autonomy in a familiar and meaningful context.

The assistive kitchen is more than just a testbed—it provides a controlled yet realistic environment where we can explore how collaboration unfolds, how users adapt to varying autonomy levels, and what design choices make robotic systems more usable and supportive in everyday life.

Robot’s Task Execution

Each robot arm is mounted on a holonomic base and manipulation tasks are learned via Imitation Learning from 70 human demonstrations. We use a flow-matching deep learning model trained on RGB point clouds. We ensure safe and reliable motion using a control barrier function based controller on top of the learned policy.

System’s Interface Devices and Control

Our platform integrates multiple input devices to enable intuitive human–robot interaction. Participants controlled the robot using a combination of EEG signals (motor imagery), EMG activity (muscle signals), and eye-tracking (gaze selection).

These signals were mapped to different robot autonomy levels: from direct teleoperation, where users managed movements step by step, to shared autonomy, where human intent guided the robot, up to full automation, where the system acted independently after goal selection.

This multimodal approach allowed us to explore how different control strategies affect performance, workload, and the overall collaboration experience.

The Shared Autonomy Study

We conducted a user study to explore how different levels of robot autonomy affect human-robot collaboration in pick-and-place tasks. A total of 18 participants were involved, divided across three experimental conditions representing low, medium, and high autonomy. Participants interacted with the robot in a controlled kitchen setting.

The study provides insights into how autonomy influences engagement, efficiency, and perceived control in shared human-robot tasks.

Results

Our study revealed clear differences across the three autonomy levels. Task performance improved with higher autonomy: SA (Level 2) and FA (Level 3) achieved higher success rates and faster completion times compared to AT (Level 1). Cognitive workload decreased as autonomy increased, with participants reporting the highest effort during AT.

Usability also improved, with SUS scores rising from AT to SA and FA. Measures of sense of agency and ownership showed that AT and SA provided more control and engagement, while FA offered efficiency at the cost of reduced direct control. Qualitative feedback highlighted that participants appreciated the balance of SA, valued FA for routine tasks, and recognized the high effort required for AT.

What We Learned

Shared Autonomy (SA, level 2) offered the most balanced experience – high task success, moderate workload, and preserved user agency.

Full Automation (FA, level 3) maximized speed and reduced effort – but users had less control, and occasional EEG mis-selections affected accuracy.

Assisted Teleoperation (AT, level 1) gave full control – but required higher mental and physical effort and longer task times.

Sense of control varied with autonomy – AT and SA gave stronger prospective agency, FA increased sense of ownership by the robot.

User feedback highlights context-dependent preferences – hybrid or adaptive systems could provide the best support, especially for tasks involving motor impairments.
